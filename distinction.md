# TabPFN-3 vs. TabICLv2: Feature-by-Feature Distinction

Comparing the two teams' *latest* releases — **TabPFN-3** (Prior Labs, May 2026) and **TabICLv2** (Inria SODA, Feb 2026) — feature by feature. For each feature: does the latest model include it, was it the first model (in either lineage) to introduce it, and if not, which earlier paper did it come from?

**Legend**: ✅ Included · ❌ Not included / not found in sources checked · ⚠️ Uncertain — inherited/likely, but not independently confirmed for this exact version in the sources checked.

> **Correction (verified against the paper's actual Figure 5 and text)**: an earlier version of this table claimed TabPFN-3 kept TabPFN v2's cell-level grid attention and did *not* adopt TabICL's two-stage architecture. That was wrong. TabPFN-3's own paper states Figure 5 is "Architecture of TabPFN-3, **adapted from the TabICLv2 architecture**," and that it "builds on the two-stage row-compression design introduced by Qu et al. ... in the TabICL architecture." The rows below now reflect this — TabPFN-3 dropped TabPFN v2's grid attention and rebuilt around TabICL's compression design, adding a TabPFN v1-flavored reasoning stage on top, plus explicitly borrowing TabICLv2's feature-grouping scheme and its "QASSMax" scalable-softmax.

| Feature | What it is (brief) | TabPFN-3 | TabICLv2 |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Core paradigm**: synthetic-data pretraining + in-context learning, with training/test masking | Model is pretrained once on millions of fake tables and never retrained; at prediction time it reads known and unknown rows together in one pass, where unknown rows may look at known rows but never vice versa. | ✅ Included.<br>Not first — inherited unchanged from **TabPFN v1** (2022), which originated the whole "Prior-Fitted Network" idea. | ✅ Included.<br>Not first — TabICL's papers explicitly credit **TabPFN v1** as originator; kept in TabICLv2's stage-2 transformer. |
| **No-padding variable-column handling** | Lets the model accept tables with any number of columns without wastefully zero-padding every table to one fixed maximum size. | ✅ Included.<br>❌ Not inherited from TabPFN v2 as previously stated — **corrected**: TabPFN-3 dropped v2's random-signature tokenizer along with the rest of v2's architecture (see grid-attention row above). Its actual mechanism now is Stage 1's inducing-point column embedding, which the paper explicitly attributes to the borrowed TabICL/TabICLv2 architecture. Predecessor: **TabICLv2** (and TabICL v1). | ✅ Included.<br>Not clearly first — **TabPFN v2** solved this ~1 month earlier via a different mechanism (the random-signature tokenizer, now abandoned even by TabPFN itself — see left column); TabICL v1's column-shape-aware network looks like independent invention under time pressure, not direct copying. Later, this TabICL-lineage mechanism is what TabPFN-3 itself adopted. |
| **Regression** (predicting a number, not just a category) | Lets the model output a numeric value (e.g. a price) instead of only a probability per class. | ✅ Included.<br>Not first — inherited from **TabPFN v2** (Jan 2025), the first version in either lineage to add it. | ✅ Included.<br>Not first — **TabPFN v2** added it a year earlier; TabICLv2 is the first TabICL version to catch up, using its own quantile-based output (see below) rather than copying TabPFN's mechanism. |
| **Native categorical & missing-value handling** | Automatically converts non-numeric columns (e.g. city names) into numbers and copes with blank cells, without manual preprocessing. | ✅ Included. Mechanism confirmed for TabPFN-3: for each cell that's missing/invalid, it computes a binary "was this missing?" flag and concatenates that flag to the (placeholder) cell value before embedding — an explicit signal rather than upstream imputation.<br>Whether this exact NaN/Inf-flag mechanism traces back to TabPFN v2 or is new in TabPFN-3 is ⚠️ not confirmed in sources checked; the *capability* (native missing-value handling) does trace back to TabPFN v2. | ❌ **Not included — corrected, now confirmed absent rather than merely unconfirmed.** TabICLv2's own Limitations section (Section 8) admits: "missing values ... are currently imputed by the mean, but remain unexplored." That's plain mean-imputation preprocessing, not a native in-model mechanism — a real, admitted gap relative to TabPFN. Native categorical handling is likewise not described; categorical values must be encoded before input. |
| **Cell-level 2D grid attention** (alternating row-wise/column-wise cross-referencing over every cell) | Represents the table as one vector per cell, then alternates letting cells compare notes across their row and across their column, layer after layer. | ❌ **Not included — corrected.** TabPFN-3 *dropped* this design. Figure 5's caption states the TabPFN-3 architecture is "adapted from the TabICLv2 architecture," replacing v2's cell-level grid attention with the two-stage row-compression scheme below. This design was unique to **TabPFN v2** and did not carry forward. | ❌ Not included — TabICL replaced this exact design with the two-stage row-compression approach below, specifically because grid attention gets too expensive on huge tables. |
| **Two-stage architecture**: compress each row into one summary vector before the actual cross-dataset reasoning step | First squashes a row's however-many columns down into a single fixed-size vector, then runs in-context learning over these row-summaries instead of over every individual cell. | ✅ **Included — corrected.** TabPFN-3's own paper states it "builds on the two-stage row-compression design introduced by Qu et al. ... in the TabICL architecture" and that Figure 5's architecture is "adapted from the TabICLv2 architecture." Not first.<br>Predecessor: **TabICL v1 / TabICLv2**, explicitly cited. TabPFN-3 then adds a Stage-3 in-context-learning transformer that the paper itself describes as "a return to TabPFN v1's" per-row-token design — so the final architecture is TabICL's compression front-end plus a TabPFN-v1-flavored reasoning stage. | ✅ Included.<br>**First** — TabICL v1's own original contribution, unchanged in TabICLv2; built specifically as a response to TabPFNv2's grid-attention cost. Later adopted by **TabPFN-3**. |
| **Feature grouping into multi-column tokens** (grouping several raw columns into one token before embedding, to cut compute on wide tables) | Instead of embedding one column per token, bundle a few columns together into a single token first, so there are fewer, chunkier items for attention to process. | ✅ Included.<br>Not first for the *general idea* — **TabPFN-2.5** first grouped 3 raw columns per token. But TabPFN-3's specific grouping scheme is explicitly borrowed: the paper states it's "adopting TabICLv2's group assignment, which creates triplets by grouping each feature with two cyclically shifted neighbors." Predecessor for this exact scheme: **TabICLv2**. | ✅ Included.<br>**First** to use this specific cyclic-shifted-triplet grouping scheme — later adopted by **TabPFN-3**. |
| **Curriculum pretraining** (progressively bigger fake tables during training) | Starts pretraining on small fake tables and gradually increases their size, rather than using huge tables from the start. | ❌ **Not included — corrected from "unconfirmed" to confirmed absent.** TabPFN-3's own synthetic-prior section (2.5) describes graph generation, combiner mechanisms, and categorical/temporal extensions, but contains no reference to progressively-sized tables during training — this specific technique was not adopted even though the surrounding architecture was. | ✅ Included.<br>Not first within TabICL's own lineage — inherited from **TabICL v1**, which introduced the three-stage size ramp-up. |
| **Broadened synthetic-data generators** (more variety of made-up rules for fake pretraining tables) | Adds new families of random mathematical relationships (curves, lines, "plateau" shapes, etc.) used to generate the fake practice tables. | ✅ **Included — now confirmed with detail, and confirmed NOT borrowed from TabICL.** TabPFN-3's Section 2.5 lists eight of its own improvements (new graph-generation methods, "combiner" mechanisms, categorical-variable handling, oscillator functions, and spatial/temporal/out-of-distribution priors), continuing and expanding TabPFN-2.5's broadening. Despite adopting TabICLv2's architecture, grouping scheme, and QASSMax, **the synthetic-data recipe itself was not borrowed** — no cross-reference to TabICLv2's prior design was found. Predecessor: **TabPFN-2.5** (own lineage only). | ✅ Included.<br>Not first as a general idea (**TabPFN v1** originated the "synthetic prior" concept itself), but TabICLv2's five specific new function families are its own addition, not borrowed from TabPFN. |
| **"Scalable softmax"** (fixing attention's tendency to blur on very long inputs) | A tweak to attention's weighting formula so focus doesn't automatically dilute just because there are more rows to compare against. | ✅ **Included — corrected.** TabPFN-3 explicitly adopts TabICLv2's version of this, called QASSMax, per the paper's own citation. Not first. Predecessor: **TabICLv2**. | ✅ Included, as "QASSMax." Not first as a totally from-scratch idea — the paper describes QASSMax as "itself inspired from SSMax," an earlier general (non-tabular) technique from outside either team. But **first within this comparison** to bring it into a tabular in-context-learning model — later adopted by **TabPFN-3**. |
| **Muon optimizer** (training-time technique) | Updates a whole layer's numbers as one matrix and evens out the directions of change, instead of adjusting each number independently like the standard AdamW method. | ⚠️ **Genuinely unknown, not confirmed absent** — checked twice against the paper's text and neither pass found any statement of which optimizer TabPFN-3 uses (AdamW, Muon, or otherwise). Don't read the earlier ❌ as a confirmed "no." | ✅ Included.<br>First *within this comparison* to bring Muon into a tabular in-context-learning model — though Muon itself is a general optimizer from the wider deep-learning world, invented by neither team. |
| **Regression uncertainty output** (a full predicted probability curve, not just one number) | Rather than a single best guess, predicts a whole spread of how likely different answers are. | ⚠️ **Partially corrected — different mechanism than TabICLv2's, not the same feature.** TabPFN-3 uses a "bar-distribution regression head": it predicts a discretized probability distribution over value buckets, then "arbitrary quantiles are decoded at inference by inverting the predicted CDF." So it *can* output quantiles, but via a different underlying mechanism than TabICLv2's, and this is **not** attributed to TabICLv2 in the sources checked — it reads as continuing prior TabPFN-lineage practice (exact origin — TabPFN v2 vs. new in TabPFN-2.5/3 — unconfirmed; Nature's paywall blocked direct verification of v2's own mechanism). | ✅ Included, via a **different, independently-developed mechanism**: predicts 999 explicit quantiles directly (via pinball loss), rather than a discretized bucket/CDF. **First** within either lineage to use this specific direct-quantile-prediction approach — not adopted by TabPFN-3, which kept its own bar-distribution approach instead. |
| **"Thinking" / test-time compute scaling** (extra computation on hard cases) | Gives the model extra room to "reason" — extra scratch rows during pretraining, or extra computation at prediction time — to improve accuracy on harder problems. | ✅ Included (as "TabPFN-3-Plus").<br>Not first within its own lineage — evolved from **TabPFN-2.5**'s "thinking rows" (Nov 2025) into a full test-time-compute variant. | ❌ Not included/not found in sources checked. |
| **KV-cache reduction, multi-query attention & row-chunking** for huge-scale inference | A bundle of memory-saving tricks — reusing one-time column summaries, storing less per-row state, sharing state across attention heads — that let the (now TabICL-derived) architecture handle far more rows without running out of memory. | ✅ Included.<br>**First** *within this comparison* — TabPFN-3's own contribution layered on top of the borrowed two-stage architecture above, though the underlying techniques (KV caching, multi-query attention) are standard tools from the general large-language-model world, invented by neither team. | ❌ Not included/not found — TabICLv2's own scaling answer remains its two-stage compression (above); no equivalent chunking/caching scheme was found in its sources. |
| **Orthogonal target/label embeddings** | Encodes the possible answer categories as vectors deliberately spread as far apart as mathematically possible before training starts (via "orthogonal decomposition" — picking non-overlapping directions), to help the model learn faster and more stably when there are many possible categories. | ✅ Included. **First** *within this comparison* — new in TabPFN-3; not attributed to TabICL in the sources checked. | ❌ Not included/not found in sources checked. |
| **Many-class decoder** (soft nearest-neighbor over in-context labels) | Instead of a fixed list of output slots (one per possible category), predicts each test row's answer as a weighted blend of whichever training rows it judged most similar — so it works for any number of categories without deciding that number in advance. | ✅ Included. **First** *within this comparison* — new in TabPFN-3; not attributed to TabICL in the sources checked. | ❌ Not included/not found in sources checked. |
| **Relational data support** (multiple linked tables) | Lets the model make predictions using data spread across more than one linked table (e.g. a customers table and an orders table). | ✅ Included (as "TabPFN-REL").<br>**First** *within this comparison* — automatically joins the linked tables into one table, then runs the ordinary model on the result. | ❌ Not included/not found in sources checked. |
| **Tabular-text support** (mixing free-text columns with numeric/categorical ones) | Lets the model take a column of free text (e.g. a product description) directly as input, alongside ordinary numeric and categorical columns. | ✅ Included (as "TabPFN-3-Plus").<br>**First** *within this comparison* — new in TabPFN-3, not present in TabPFN-2.5. | ❌ **Not included — confirmed absent, not just unfound.** TabICLv2's own Limitations section (Section 8) states it "does not natively leverage semantic information from column names or textual features," and suggests only a post-hoc combination with separate text embeddings as future work. |
| **Time-series extension** | A specialized version for forecasting a sequence of values over time (e.g. tomorrow's sales from past days), rather than an unordered table. | ✅ Included (as "TabPFN-TS-3").<br>Not first within its own lineage — continues an existing TabPFN time-series spinoff (TabPFN-TS) that predates TabPFN-2.5/3. | ⚠️ A related TabICL time-series spinoff exists but is a separate release from the core TabICLv2 paper.<br>Not first — its own authors call it "heavily inspired by" **TabPFN-TS**, i.e. explicitly borrowed from the rival lineage. |
| **Distillation into compact deployable models** (small MLP / tree ensemble) | A tool for compressing the big transformer down into a much smaller, faster model that approximates its predictions, for easier deployment. | ✅ **Included — now confirmed, not just inherited-and-assumed.** The paper states directly: "TabPFN-3 also supports distillation into dataset-specific MLPs or tree ensembles via the engine introduced with TabPFN-2.5." Not first. Predecessor: **TabPFN-2.5** (own lineage). | ❌ Not included in the model itself — **confirmed, with detail**: TabICLv2's related-work section cites distillation of tabular foundation models as an existing technique (citing, among others, Grinsztajn et al. 2025 — TabPFN-2.5's own distillation work) but does not implement it itself. |
| **FlashAttention & multi-GPU engineering speedups** | Adopting faster, more memory-efficient ways of computing the exact same attention math (no change to predictions, only speed), plus splitting work across GPUs. | ✅ **Included — corrected and expanded.** TabPFN-3 "ships with two opt-in performance features: torch.compile ... and FlashAttention-3 (FA3), a Hopper-specific kernel for the in-context-learning attention." FlashAttention-3 was first adopted into the TabPFN lineage by **TabPFN-2.5** (Nov 2025); TabPFN-3 continues it and adds torch.compile on top. | ✅ **Included — corrected: this was wrongly marked "not included."** TabICL v1's own paper states "FlashAttention and automatic mixed precision are applied globally," citing FlashAttention's "linear memory complexity" as central to its efficiency — used since **TabICL v1** (Feb 2025), i.e. before TabPFN's lineage adopted it. TabICLv2 discusses (but doesn't fully resolve in the sources checked) staying compatible with "the FlashAttention ecosystem" when choosing its QASSMax implementation, suggesting continuity from v1. <br><br>Net effect: **FlashAttention itself was invented by neither team** (Dao et al., 2022, outside both lineages) and was adopted independently by TabICL (Feb 2025) and by TabPFN (starting Nov 2025 with TabPFN-2.5) — this looks like convergent adoption of a standard industry tool, not cross-team copying. |

## Notes on how to read this

- "First" means first across **either** team's full history (TabPFN v1/v2/2.5/3 and TabICL v1/v1.1/v2), not just first within the model's own lineage.
- Several cells are marked ⚠️ because the underlying research (abstract- and methods-section reads of the arXiv papers/technical reports) did not explicitly confirm that detail for that exact version — this reflects a gap in what was findable in the sources checked, not a confirmed absence.
- Where a feature's *concept* originated in one lineage but the *specific mechanism* differs (e.g. no-padding column handling, regression), both are noted so the distinction between "same idea" and "same implementation" isn't lost.
- **The overall cross-pollination picture is bidirectional, not one-way.** TabPFN-3's paper directly cites Qu et al.'s TabICL and TabICLv2 papers multiple times and explicitly names TabICLv2 as its architectural basis, its feature-grouping source, and its scalable-softmax (QASSMax) source — and it benchmarks extensively against TabICLv2 in its experiments section. This reverses the impression (from an earlier, shallower reading of only the abstract) that borrowing ran solely from TabICL toward TabPFN. See `research_icons.md`'s Cross-pollination section, now corrected to match.
- **The awareness (benchmarking) started a generation earlier than the architectural borrowing.** TabPFN-2.5 (Nov 2025) already lists TabICL (v1) as an experimental comparison baseline — so TabPFN was tracking TabICL's numbers a full release before TabPFN-3 adopted TabICL's architecture outright. TabPFN-2.5's own feature-grouping (group-of-3 columns) carries no external citation in its own paper, i.e. it reads as independently developed, not yet borrowed — the architectural borrowing specifically starts at TabPFN-3, not TabPFN-2.5.
- **Not everything was borrowed — TabPFN-3 kept several pieces of its own lineage unchanged**: its synthetic-data generator (Section 2.5's eight improvements, no TabICLv2 cross-reference), its "bar-distribution" regression-uncertainty mechanism (different from TabICLv2's direct-quantile approach), its distillation engine (from TabPFN-2.5), and its new orthogonal-label-embedding / many-class-decoder pieces (which appear to be TabPFN-3 originals). The picture is selective adoption of TabICLv2's architecture plus continued independent work elsewhere, not a wholesale merger.
- A genuine surprise from double-checking: **FlashAttention turned out to be used by both models, contradicting an earlier (wrong) row that said TabICLv2 didn't use it** — TabICL has used it since v1 (Feb 2025), predating TabPFN's own adoption starting at TabPFN-2.5 (Nov 2025). Since FlashAttention is a general external technique (Dao et al., 2022), this looks like independent convergent adoption rather than borrowing in either direction.
