## Ground rules / definitions used throughout

- **Table**: rows (individual examples — e.g. patients, houses, customers) and columns (measurements about each example — e.g. age, price, income). One column is usually the **target** — the thing you're trying to predict.
- **Vector**: just a list of numbers. **Matrix**: a grid of numbers. A **linear layer** is the most basic building block of a neural network: it turns an input vector into an output vector by multiplying it by a matrix (and usually adding a fixed extra vector, called a bias) — nothing fancier than matrix multiplication.
- **Token / embedding**: before a transformer can process something (a row, a single cell, a word), that thing has to first become a vector. Turning it into a vector — usually via a linear layer, sometimes with some learned extra numbers mixed in — is called "embedding" it, and the resulting vector is its "token."
- **In-context learning**: instead of *learning a formula* from your specific table (what normal machine learning does — e.g. fitting a line to points), you hand the model your whole table — rows where you already know the answer ("training rows"), plus new rows where you don't ("test rows") — in one shot, and it reads all of it at once and guesses the missing answers, the way you'd guess a pattern from a handful of solved examples sitting next to the new problem.
- **Attention / "cross-referencing"**: the core operation inside a transformer. Every token (vector) in a list gets to look at every other token in the list and compute a weighted average of them, where the *weights* (how much attention to pay to each other token) are themselves computed by the network based on how relevant two tokens look to each other. Each token then updates itself using that weighted average. Stack many of these steps ("layers") on top of each other and the tokens gradually refine their understanding of each other. I call this "cross-referencing" throughout.
- **Attention mask**: a rule about which tokens are *allowed* to cross-reference which other tokens. E.g. "test rows may look at training rows, but training rows may never look at test rows" is a mask — it stops information about the answer you're trying to predict from leaking backward into the rows that are supposed to represent ground truth.
- **Softmax**: the specific formula attention uses to turn a list of raw relevance scores into weights that sum to 1 (so they form a proper weighted average): take each score, exponentiate it (i.e. raise a fixed number > 1 to that power, which makes bigger scores balloon up disproportionately), then divide each by the sum of all of them. This is why attention can be "sharp" (concentrating almost all weight on one or two highly relevant tokens) rather than just a plain, uniform average.
- **Prior / synthetic data generator**: since these models are pretrained purely on made-up tables, "the prior" means the recipe used to invent those tables. Two common recipes appear below:
  - **Structural causal model (SCM)**: picture a flowchart of made-up variables, where an arrow from variable A to variable B means "B's value is computed from A's value, plus some randomness." You build a random flowchart like this, then generate many rows by re-rolling all the randomness and reading off the resulting variable values each time — some of those variables become your fake table's columns, one becomes the fake target.
  - **Random neural network (as a "Bayesian neural network prior")**: instead of a flowchart, you build a small neural network and fill its matrices with random numbers (rather than training them). This random network isn't being trained — it's being used as a fixed, arbitrary, but internally-consistent function. You feed it random inputs (plus a bit of injected noise) and whatever numbers come out the other end become the fake labels.
- **Quantile**: sort every possible answer from smallest to largest; the *p%* quantile is the value below which exactly *p%* of that sorted list falls. The 50% quantile is the median. Predicting several quantiles instead of one number gives you a whole probability curve for the answer instead of just a single guess.
- **KV cache**: when a transformer cross-references test rows against training rows, it doesn't need to redo the training-side computation for every single new test row — it can compute the training rows' contribution once, store ("cache") it, and reuse it. What gets stored is called the "key/value" (KV) cache.

## The timeline

### 1. TabPFN v1 — July 5, 2022 (arXiv:2207.01848)
**Authors**: Hollmann, Müller, Eggensperger, Hutter (University of Freiburg) · Published ICLR 2023

✨ The paper that started the field: the first working "read the whole table at once" in-context-learning model for tables, called a **Prior-Fitted Network (PFN)**.

🔧 **Mechanism, concretely:**
- **Turning rows into tokens**: every row's feature values are zero-padded out to a fixed length (up to 100 numbers) and then run through *one shared linear layer* to produce that row's token vector. The row's label (for training rows) is embedded too. So the "network" here is a fairly ordinary transformer — its distinguishing trick isn't its per-layer math, it's what it's trained on and how the tokens are masked (next point).
- **Attention mask**: training-row tokens cross-reference each other freely (like a normal transformer). Test-row tokens are allowed to cross-reference the training rows, but training rows never cross-reference test rows, and test rows never cross-reference each other. This means: (a) the training rows' internal representations never change depending on which test point you're predicting, and (b) you can hand the model many test rows at once and get every one of their predictions out of a *single* forward pass, because they don't interfere with each other.
- **The "prior" (how the fake training tables are invented)**: two recipes, mixed roughly evenly. (1) **Structural causal models** — random flowcharts of made-up variables as described above, where each variable is some function of its flowchart-parents plus noise; re-running the randomness many times generates the fake rows. (2) **Random ("Bayesian") neural networks** — a neural net with randomly-drawn (untrained) weights, used as a fixed random function: random inputs go in, and whatever numbers happen to come out the other end (after adding some noise) become the fake labels. Across pretraining, the network sees literally millions of these fake tables and learns the general skill of "given some rows with known answers, guess the answer for a new row" — not any specific real-world pattern.
- **Producing predictions**: after cross-referencing, each test row's final token vector is run through a small classification head (another linear layer) to produce a probability for each possible class — and because all test rows were processed together in the same forward pass, you get every prediction at once.

Two big limits: ❌ **classification only** — the output head above only ever produces "probability of each category," there's no mechanism here for predicting a plain number. ❌ **fixed max table size** (~1,000 rows, ~100 columns) — because every row has to be squeezed through the *same* fixed-length linear layer regardless of how many real columns it has, tables with fewer columns waste padding slots, and no table can exceed the fixed slot count at all. It also means column 3 is always processed the same way whether it holds "age" or "price" — the model has no way to tell columns apart except by their position in the padded vector.

### 2. TabPFN v2 — online January 8, 2025, *Nature* Vol. 637, pp. 319–326 (DOI 10.1038/s41586-024-08328-6)
**Authors**: Hollmann, Müller, Purucker, Krishnakumar, Körfer, Hoo, Schirrmeister, Hutter

⭐ Explicitly framed as fixing v1's "many limitations that rendered it inapplicable in most cases." Rather than tweaking v1's one-token-per-row design, it changes what a token *is*.

🔧 **Mechanism, concretely:**
- **Cell-level tokens, not row-level tokens.** The whole table (rows × columns, plus one extra slot each for the label and a placeholder) is represented as a grid of individual vectors — one vector *per cell*, not one per row. This is the key structural change from v1.
- **Two attention passes per layer, alternating.** In each layer, the model first lets every cell cross-reference every *other cell in the same row* (i.e. across that row's columns), and then, separately, lets every cell cross-reference every *other cell in the same column* (i.e. across that column's rows). Repeating this "across, then down" pair of passes over many layers lets information mix along both directions of the table without ever needing to flatten a whole row into one fixed-size vector the way v1 did.
- ⭐ **The column tokenizer, replacing zero-padding.** Each column *j* gets a fixed random vector assigned to it once per dataset; that random vector is passed through a small learned matrix to produce a "signature" vector for column *j*. Every cell in column *j* is then embedded as (that cell's numeric value, scaled) plus column *j*'s signature vector. Because the signature is shared by every cell in the same column but random-and-different across columns, cells anywhere in the table can now be told apart by *which column they came from*, without ever needing to know in advance what that column means, or padding anything to a fixed maximum count. This is what removes v1's 100-column ceiling — the model just processes however many column-signatures are actually present.
- **Categorical columns**: rather than a fundamentally new internal mechanism, categorical values (e.g. a "city name" column) are converted into numbers first — via ordinal encoding (each category gets assigned an integer) or one-hot encoding (a separate 0/1 column per category) — before entering the same numeric pipeline described above. "Handles categorical data natively" mainly means this conversion happens automatically for you rather than requiring the model to have a bespoke categorical-only pathway.
- ⚠️ **Missing values and regression output**: our sources confirm the *capabilities* (native missing-value handling; a single-number regression output produced via a small output head on the test row's final vector) but not the precise internal representation used for each — e.g. whether a special learned "this value is missing" vector is substituted in for missing cells. Treat the exact wording of this mechanism as unconfirmed, though the capability itself is solid (confirmed by the *Nature* paper and independent corroboration).
- 📈 **Result of all this**: scales ~50x versus v1, to **10,000 rows and 500 columns**, plus ✨ **regression** as a genuinely new capability (v1's output head only ever produced class probabilities) and ⭐ far more robustness to noisy/irrelevant columns and outliers than v1.

### 3. TabICL v1 — submitted February 8, 2025, revised May 24, 2025 (arXiv:2502.05564)
**Authors**: Qu, Holzmüller, Varoquaux, Le Morvan (Inria SODA team) · ICML 2025

🔗 A different team enters, and says so directly: TabPFNv2 "largely improves TabPFN in terms of both prediction performance and scalability," but ❌ its two-attention-passes-per-layer design above — repeated over the *full* row-by-column grid, at every one of many layers — gets prohibitively expensive as the table grows, because the amount of work grows with (rows × columns) at every single layer. Their fix, building explicitly on TabPFN's whole approach:

🔧 **Mechanism, concretely — a genuine two-stage architecture:**
- **Stage 1a — embed each cell using its whole column's shape.** For each column, a small transformer (call it the "column network") looks at every value in that column across all rows, treated as an unordered set (it doesn't matter what order the rows come in for this step). From this, it learns a personalized scale and shift *for that column specifically* — in effect, "here's how to normalize numbers that look like they came from this particular column's distribution." Every individual cell value is then embedded using that column-specific scale and shift. Concretely: this lets the model reasonably embed a column that's mostly small fractions completely differently from a column that's mostly in the thousands, purely by looking at the shape of that column's own numbers — it never needs to be told in advance what the column "means."
- **Stage 1b — compress each row into one fixed-size vector.** Now every row has one embedded vector per column. A second transformer cross-references across *only that row's own column-vectors* (attention within the row, across its columns) — plus four extra learned "helper" vectors are added into this cross-referencing step, and after cross-referencing, their four resulting outputs are concatenated together into a single, fixed-size (512-number) summary vector for that row — regardless of whether the row originally had 5 columns or 500. This is the step that removes the need for zero-padding entirely: because the column network in stage 1a and the row-compressor in stage 1b both just operate on "however many column-vectors are actually present," a variable number of columns is handled naturally, the same way a recipe that says "whisk all the eggs in the bowl" works whether there are 2 eggs or 12.
- **Stage 2 — the actual in-context learning, on summaries instead of raw cells.** A standard 12-layer transformer, using the exact same training-rows-cross-reference-each-other / test-rows-only-look-at-training-rows masking scheme as TabPFN, but now every row is *one* 512-number vector instead of a whole row of individual cell-vectors. This is the entire point of the two-stage design: cross-referencing 500,000 short summary vectors against each other is far cheaper than TabPFN v2's approach of cross-referencing every individual cell against every other cell in a 500,000-row × 500-column grid, at every layer.
- ⭐📈⚡ **Curriculum training and the resulting numbers**: pretraining deliberately starts with fake tables fixed at 1,000 rows, then random sizes between 1,000–40,000 rows, then random sizes between 40,000–60,000 rows — gradually stretching the model's row-count "muscle" instead of throwing huge tables at it from the very start. The payoff: usable at prediction time on tables up to **500,000 rows and 500 columns**, 1.5x–10x faster than TabPFNv2 depending on size, matching its accuracy on smaller tables and beating both TabPFNv2 and the tree-based method CatBoost on the largest tables tested.
- ❌ Still classification only, like TabPFN v1 — stage 2's output head, like v1's, only ever produces class probabilities.

### 4. TabICL v1.1 — May 2025 (unpublished checkpoint, no paper)

⭐ A quiet update: the exact same TabICL v1 architecture above, just re-trained further on an early draft of what would become v2's improved training-data recipe (see TabICLv2 below for what that recipe actually changed). No architectural change here — ❌ still classification-only, no new paper.

### 5. TabPFN-2.5 — submitted November 11, 2025, revised February 5, 2026 (arXiv:2511.08667)
**Authors**: Grinsztajn, Flöge, Key, et al., including Hollmann and Hutter

🔗 TabPFN's answer to the scale problem TabICL had been pointing at — and unlike TabICL's answer (a two-stage architecture that changes what gets cross-referenced), TabPFN-2.5 keeps v2's cell-level, two-attention-pass-per-layer design and instead attacks the problem from several other directions at once:

🔧 **Mechanism, concretely — five separate changes bundled together:**
1. ⭐ **A deeper network.** More cross-referencing layers than v2: 18 layers for the regression version, 24 for the classification version — simply more rounds of refinement per prediction.
2. ⚡ **Coarser feature grouping.** Instead of embedding one raw column per token slot (as v2's tokenizer effectively does), TabPFN-2.5 groups 3 raw columns together into a single token before embedding (versus grouping pairs of 2 previously). Fewer, chunkier tokens means less work for the cross-referencing steps on wide tables.
3. ⭐ **Richer synthetic training data.** Broadens the variety of made-up statistical rules used to generate the millions of fake practice tables, and generates bigger fake tables during pretraining than v2 did — directly exposing the model to more of the scale and diversity it'll face in the real world.
4. ⚡ **Pure engineering speedups, not new math.** Adopts FlashAttention-3 — a way of computing the *exact same* cross-referencing weighted averages more efficiently in terms of memory movement, changing nothing about what's computed, only how fast — plus splits the work across multiple GPUs in parallel. Also applies a menu of column preprocessing steps (robust scaling, soft-clipping extreme outliers, a quantile-based transform, and an SVD-based transform — SVD being a standard way of re-expressing a matrix's numbers along its most informative directions) before columns even reach v2's tokenizer, giving the model better-prepared numbers to work with.
5. ✨ **"Thinking rows."** Adds 64 extra, entirely artificial rows to the input table alongside your real rows, purely as reusable scratch space — during pretraining the network learns what's useful to write into these extra rows to help itself reason, and they're discarded before you see the final answer. This is the same idea as giving yourself extra scratch paper for a hard calculation, except the network decides for itself what to write there.

📈 **Result of all this bundle**: pushes the table-size ceiling to **50,000 rows and 2,000 columns** — about 20x more total table cells than TabPFNv2 could handle.

🔗 **Also directly benchmarks against TabICL** (v1) as an experimental comparison baseline — this is the first documented point of direct cross-team engagement in the sources checked, a full generation *before* TabPFN-3 went on to adopt TabICL's architecture outright. At this point, though, the borrowing is still just benchmarking/awareness: TabPFN-2.5's feature-grouping (item 2 above) carries no external citation in its own paper, i.e. it reads as independently developed, not yet architecturally influenced by TabICL.

> ⚠️ Caveat: the paper's claim of thereby leading a particular leaderboard (TabArena) did **not** hold up under our verification pass and is omitted as unconfirmed. The 20x scale-up figure and the five mechanisms above are well-supported.

### 6. TabICLv2 — submitted February 11, 2026 (arXiv:2602.11139)
**Authors**: Qu, Holzmüller, Varoquaux, Le Morvan (same team) · ICML 2026

🔗⚡ Builds on TabICL v1's two-stage design unchanged, and explicitly benchmarks against TabPFN's newest release (TabPFN-2.5), claiming up to 10x faster prediction on a 50,000-row table on the same hardware. Four concrete changes:

🔧 **Mechanism, concretely:**
1. ⭐ **"Scalable softmax," fixing a real mathematical problem.** Recall softmax turns raw relevance scores into weights that sum to 1 by exponentiating and dividing by the total. As a table gets bigger, there are simply more rows to cross-reference against, so that total (the denominator) grows too — which mathematically forces the resulting weights to spread out thinner across more items, purely as a side effect of table size, even when the truly relevant rows haven't changed. In other words: attention gets "blurrier" on bigger tables for reasons that have nothing to do with relevance. TabICLv2's fix rescales each row's query vector before the relevance scores are even computed, using a small learned adjustment that depends on the logarithm of how many rows are being cross-referenced against, plus a second learned per-row adjustment — so the model can automatically "sharpen its focus" by a learned amount to compensate whenever the group it's cross-referencing against is larger, rather than passively drifting toward a blurry average.
2. ⭐ **A more varied fake-data recipe.** Adds five new families of random rules for generating fake columns and their relationships (smooth random curves from Gaussian processes, straight-line functions, curved/quadratic functions, "plateau"-shaped functions, and combinations of these) on top of TabICL v1's original recipe, plus a redesigned way of drawing the random causal flowchart connecting columns together (allowing more general, tangled flowchart shapes rather than only simple tree-like ones), and it now filters out and discards any randomly generated flowchart where the target column ends up completely unrelated to the input columns — since practicing on a "table" where there's genuinely nothing to learn wastes training time.
3. ⭐ **A different training optimizer: Muon instead of AdamW.** AdamW (the standard choice almost everywhere) adjusts each individual number in the network based on that number's own recent history of updates, one at a time. Muon instead looks at an entire layer's update matrix all at once and rescales it so that no single especially-large direction of change dominates the others — roughly, it evens out how much different "directions" the network could move in get to influence one update step. This lets training take bigger, more stable steps.
4. ✨ **Regression via predicted quantiles, catching up to a capability TabPFN v2 added a year earlier** (❌ this had been a TabICL weakness since v1). Rather than predicting one number, the model predicts 999 different quantiles at once (the 0.1%, 0.2%, ... 99.9% quantiles). Those 999 numbers are then forced to be non-decreasing (sorted, if they came out slightly out of order) and stretched outward at the extreme ends to cover rare far-off values, which together trace out a full predicted probability curve for the answer — not just a single best guess, but a whole range with likelihoods attached. ⚠️ This turns out to be a genuinely different mechanism from TabPFN-3's later "bar-distribution" approach (predicting a distribution over buckets rather than 999 direct quantile points) — not something TabPFN-3 ended up copying, despite copying several other TabICLv2 pieces.
5. ⭐🔗 **"Repeated feature grouping," a new, explicitly original grouping scheme.** The paper states: "We propose *repeated feature grouping*, which places each feature into multiple groups via circular shifts while preserving the number of effective features," contrasting this with the simpler grouping used by "TabPFNv2 and TabPFN-2.5" (which just bundles fixed sets of 2–3 columns together, without the circular-shift/multiple-membership trick). This later became one of the specific pieces **TabPFN-3 explicitly adopted** (see below) — a case of TabICLv2 improving on a TabPFN idea, which TabPFN then adopted back.

⚠️ **Two confirmed limitations, from the paper's own Section 8 ("Limitations")**: (1) missing values are "currently imputed by the mean, but remain unexplored" — i.e. simple preprocessing, not a native in-model mechanism (unlike TabPFN's NaN/Inf-flag approach); (2) it "does not natively leverage semantic information from column names or textual features" — no text-column support, unlike TabPFN-3-Plus's later tabular-text extension.

⚡ **FlashAttention, used since TabICL v1** (not a TabICLv2 novelty): the original TabICL v1 paper states "FlashAttention and automatic mixed precision are applied globally," citing its linear memory scaling as central to TabICL's efficiency — this predates TabPFN's lineage adopting the same external tool (TabPFN-2.5, a full 9 months later, Nov 2025).

### 7. TabPFN-3 — submitted May 13, 2026, revised May 28, 2026 (arXiv:2605.13986)
**Authors**: Grinsztajn (lead) et al., 41-author Prior Labs technical report including Hollmann, Müller, Schölkopf, LeCun, Hutter

📈 Another big scale jump: up to **1,000,000 rows and 200 columns**, on a single GPU.

> ⚠️ **Correction**: an earlier version of this document said TabPFN-3 kept TabPFN v2's cell-level grid attention (row-wise/column-wise alternating attention) and scaled it up with chunking. That was wrong. On checking the actual paper (Figure 5 and its caption), TabPFN-3 **replaced** v2's architecture entirely with a design explicitly borrowed from TabICL. The corrected mechanism follows.

🔗🔧 **Mechanism, concretely — TabPFN-3 openly rebuilds its architecture around TabICL's design:**
- 🔗 **The headline fact.** Figure 5's caption reads: "Architecture of TabPFN-3, **adapted from the TabICLv2 architecture**." The paper's own text says TabPFN-3 "builds on the two-stage row-compression design introduced by Qu et al. ... in the TabICL architecture." This is an explicit, named, cited architectural adoption — not an independent parallel invention.
- ⭐ **Three stages, replacing v2's cell-level grid attention:**
  1. **Stage 1 — column-wise feature-distribution embedding.** Each column is embedded using an efficient "inducing-point" attention mechanism (a standard trick for summarizing a large set of numbers cheaply, conceptually similar to TabICL's column network) that captures the shape of that column's values without the cost scaling directly with how many rows there are.
  2. **Stage 2 — row-wise feature aggregation.** For each row, a set of 4 learned "helper" vectors (cls tokens) and that row's embedded columns cross-reference each other; the 4 outputs are concatenated into one fixed-size vector per row — this is TabICL's row-compression idea, adopted directly.
  3. **Stage 3 — in-context learning over row vectors.** Training-row vectors cross-reference each other; test-row vectors cross-reference only the training-row vectors, to produce predictions — the paper describes this stage as "a return to TabPFN v1's" original per-row-token design. So the net effect: TabICL's compression front-end (stages 1–2) feeds into a TabPFN-v1-flavored reasoning stage (stage 3), and v2's cell-level grid attention is dropped entirely.
- ⭐🔗 **Feature grouping, also borrowed from TabICLv2.** Before stage 1, features are grouped into triplets — but not just any grouping: the paper says it's "adopting TabICLv2's group assignment, which creates triplets by grouping each feature with two cyclically shifted neighbors" (grouping feature *i* with features some fixed distance away, cycling around, rather than just adjacent triples).
- ⭐🔗 **"QASSMax," also borrowed from TabICLv2.** The paper explicitly uses TabICLv2's scalable-softmax fix (itself inspired by an earlier, more general technique called SSMax from outside either team) to keep attention from blurring on huge tables.
- ✨ **Orthogonal target embeddings.** Training labels are encoded using vectors initialized to be as mutually "spread apart" as possible (via orthogonal decomposition — picking a set of directions that don't overlap with each other, the same idea as picking perpendicular axes), which helps training stay stable when there are many possible output categories. This appears to be a genuine TabPFN-3 original contribution.
- ✨ **Many-class decoder.** Rather than a fixed number of output slots (one per possible category, decided in advance), predictions are computed as a weighted blend of the actual training-row labels that a test row's vector most resembles — like literally averaging together the labels of your closest lookalikes, weighted by how closely they resemble you. This lets the model handle any number of categories without deciding that number ahead of time. Also appears to be a genuine TabPFN-3 original contribution.
- ✅ **Native missing/invalid-value handling, mechanism now confirmed.** For any cell that's missing or invalid, the model computes a simple yes/no flag ("was this missing?") and appends that flag to the cell's (placeholder) value before embedding it — so the model gets an explicit signal rather than having a stand-in number quietly substituted in.
- ⚡ **Row-chunking + reduced KV cache**, now correctly understood as scaling tricks *on top of* the three-stage design above, not on top of v2's grid attention: the one-time column summaries from stage 1 are computed once over the whole training set and reused for every chunk of rows processed afterward; only the training-side keys/values from stage 3's cross-referencing are kept (not the test-side ones, since they're not needed again); and one shared "note format" is used across attention heads when comparing test rows to training rows (multi-query attention) instead of a separate one per head. Together, this is what lets 1,000,000 rows fit on a single GPU.
- ⚡ **FlashAttention-3 and torch.compile, continuing/expanding from TabPFN-2.5.** The paper describes shipping "two opt-in performance features: torch.compile, which fuses dispatch on the non-attention hot paths, and FlashAttention-3 (FA3), a Hopper-specific kernel for the in-context-learning attention." FlashAttention-3 was already adopted starting with TabPFN-2.5; torch.compile fusion is new to v3. ⚠️ Note: TabICL has used ordinary FlashAttention since its *own* v1 (Feb 2025) — earlier than TabPFN's lineage adopted it (Nov 2025) — so this looks like both teams independently converging on a standard external tool (from Dao et al., 2022, outside either team) rather than either side copying the other.
- ✅ **Regression uncertainty output, mechanism now confirmed — and it's a different mechanism than TabICLv2's.** TabPFN-3 uses a "bar-distribution regression head": rather than directly predicting quantiles the way TabICLv2 does, it predicts a discretized probability distribution over value buckets, and "arbitrary quantiles are decoded at inference by inverting the predicted CDF ... all from a single forward pass." This capability is treated by the paper as existing prior art (not flagged as new), so it likely continues earlier TabPFN-lineage practice rather than being borrowed from TabICL — this specific piece was *not* found to be adopted from TabICLv2.
- ❌ **No curriculum pretraining, confirmed absent (not just unconfirmed).** TabPFN-3's synthetic-prior section (2.5) describes graph generation, "combiner" mechanisms, and categorical/temporal extensions, but never mentions progressively-sized tables during training — unlike TabICL's lineage, which has used a size ramp-up since TabICL v1.
- ✅ **Broadened synthetic-data generator, confirmed as TabPFN-3's own work, not borrowed.** Section 2.5 lists eight of its own improvements (new graph-generation methods, combiner mechanisms, categorical-variable handling, oscillator functions, and spatial/temporal/out-of-distribution priors) — continuing TabPFN-2.5's broadening. Despite adopting TabICLv2's architecture, grouping scheme, and QASSMax, the underlying data-generation recipe was kept as TabPFN's own; no cross-reference to TabICLv2's five new function families was found.
- ✅ **Distillation into compact models, now confirmed rather than assumed.** The paper states directly: "TabPFN-3 also supports distillation into dataset-specific MLPs or tree ensembles via the engine introduced with TabPFN-2.5" — a continued, not new, capability.
- ✨ **Relational data (TabPFN-REL).** Rather than a new cross-table attention mechanism, this works by first automatically flattening multiple linked tables into one combined table (the standard database "join" operation, done for you), then running the three-stage model above on that one resulting table.
- ✨ **Tabular-text (TabPFN-3-Plus).** Accepts text-valued columns (e.g. a free-text product description) directly, and embeds them jointly with the numeric/categorical columns *inside* the same model, so cross-feature interactions between text and structured columns get learned end-to-end. ⚠️ The exact internal mechanism for turning text into a vector isn't specified in the sources found — treat this detail as unconfirmed. This is a real capability gap versus TabICLv2, whose own Limitations section admits it "does not natively leverage semantic information from column names or textual features."
- ⚠️ **"Thinking" variant (TabPFN-3-Plus test-time compute).** Applies additional computation at prediction time to improve accuracy on harder cases, reportedly outperforming other models "by over 200 Elo" as a result — but the exact form this extra computation takes isn't detailed in the sources found. Treat the mechanism as unconfirmed; the reported effect is not.
- ❓ **Optimizer**: not disclosed in the sources checked (double-checked twice) — whether TabPFN-3 uses AdamW, Muon (which TabICLv2 introduced), or something else remains unknown.
- 🔗 **Benchmarks directly and extensively against TabICLv2**, including as the primary comparison point on its large-data (100K–1M row) benchmark and on a time-series benchmark (fev-bench) — this is the opposite of the earlier (incorrect) claim that TabPFN-3 doesn't engage with TabICL at all. Notably, this cross-team *benchmarking* awareness actually starts one generation earlier: **TabPFN-2.5's own paper already lists TabICL (v1) as an experimental comparison baseline**, a full release before TabPFN-3 went on to adopt TabICL's architecture outright.

## Cross-pollination between the two teams

> ⚠️ **Correction**: an earlier version of this section concluded that borrowing ran only from TabICL toward TabPFN, and that "TabPFN's papers don't engage with TabICL's architectural ideas directly." That conclusion was based on an abstract-only reading of TabPFN-3 and was wrong. A full read of TabPFN-3's Methods section (specifically Figure 5 and its caption) shows the opposite: TabPFN-3's architecture is explicitly, directly adapted from TabICLv2. The corrected picture is below.

This isn't two teams working in isolation — it's an open, running, **two-way** back-and-forth, and by the latest generation it runs strongly in both directions:

- 🔗 **TabICL → TabPFN, at the paradigm level (2025).** TabICL's first paper (2025) opens by crediting TabPFN as the originator of the "read the whole table at once" paradigm, and frames its own two-stage design purely as a fix for TabPFNv2's scaling bottleneck. TabICLv2 again names "TabPFNv2 and TabICL" together as the two models that "dethroned" traditional tree-based methods, and directly benchmarks against TabPFN's latest release each time. TabICL's time-series forecasting spin-off is described by its own authors as "heavily inspired by" TabPFN's own time-series variant, TabPFN-TS.
- 🔗 **TabICL → TabPFN, at the architecture level (2026) — the bigger finding.** TabPFN-3's own paper states its Figure 5 architecture is "adapted from the TabICLv2 architecture," explicitly citing Qu et al.'s TabICL and TabICLv2 papers, and says it "builds on the two-stage row-compression design introduced by Qu et al." TabPFN-3 also explicitly adopts TabICLv2's specific feature-grouping scheme ("adopting TabICLv2's group assignment") and its scalable-softmax mechanism, QASSMax. In other words: TabPFN abandoned its own v2-era cell-level grid attention design and rebuilt around TabICL's architecture, adding back a TabPFN-v1-flavored reasoning stage on top. TabPFN-3 also benchmarks extensively against TabICLv2 throughout its experiments (its primary comparison point on large-data and time-series benchmarks).
- **TabPFN → TabICL.** TabPFN set the original paradigm (Row 1) and the original per-column, no-padding fix (TabPFN v2, about a month before TabICL v1's own different mechanism for the same problem). TabICLv2's "repeated feature grouping" is explicitly presented as an improvement over the simpler grouping used by "TabPFNv2 and TabPFN-2.5" — so TabICL does engage with and build past specific TabPFN mechanisms, just not by directly copying them wholesale the way TabPFN-3 later copied TabICL's architecture.
- **The benchmarking/awareness started earlier than the architectural borrowing.** TabPFN-2.5 (Nov 2025) already lists TabICL (v1) as a comparison baseline in its own experiments — a full generation before TabPFN-3 (May 2026) adopted TabICL's architecture outright. So the relationship moved from "aware of and measured against" (TabPFN-2.5 era) to "directly rebuilt around" (TabPFN-3).
- **Selective, not total, convergence.** TabPFN-3 kept several of its own lineage's pieces unchanged even while adopting TabICLv2's architecture: its synthetic-data generator, its bar-distribution regression-uncertainty mechanism (distinct from TabICLv2's direct-quantile approach), its distillation engine, and its new orthogonal-label-embedding / many-class-decoder additions all remain its own, with no found TabICL cross-reference.
- **FlashAttention is a case of independent convergence, not borrowing.** TabICL has used it since v1 (Feb 2025); TabPFN's lineage adopted it starting at TabPFN-2.5 (Nov 2025). Since it's a general external tool (Dao et al., 2022) invented by neither team, this reads as both teams independently reaching for the same industry-standard efficiency trick, not cross-team copying.

**Bottom line, corrected**: TabPFN set the initial paradigm; TabICL responded with a genuinely novel architecture (the two-stage row-compression) built specifically to fix a cost problem in TabPFNv2's design, and refined specific TabPFN mechanisms further (feature grouping) along the way; TabPFN then tracked TabICL's numbers for a generation (TabPFN-2.5) before, rather than sticking with its own architecture and merely out-scaling TabICL through engineering (the earlier, incorrect conclusion), **the latest TabPFN generation openly adopted TabICL's architecture wholesale** (TabPFN-3), layering TabPFN-flavored additions (a v1-style reasoning stage, orthogonal label embeddings, a many-class decoder, relational/text/time-series extensions, its own memory-efficiency tricks, and a kept-independent data generator and regression mechanism) on top of it. This is a much more genuinely bidirectional, converging relationship than "TabPFN leads, TabICL catches up" — by 2026 the two architectures are converging toward a shared design, while each side still keeps some pieces independent.

## Caveats and open questions

⚠️ Two claims were explicitly refuted during adversarial verification and excluded from this report:
1. TabPFN-2.5's 20x scale-up "enabling it to lead the TabArena benchmark" (the scale-up figure is corroborated; the benchmark-leadership claim is not).
2. A TabPFN v2 author list that included "Eddie Bergman" (the correct 8-author list per *Nature*/Crossref does not include this name).

⚠️ Several mechanism details above are marked unconfirmed at the point they appear (TabPFN v2's exact missing-value/regression internals — Nature's paywall blocked direct verification on two separate attempts; TabPFN-3's text-embedding mechanism; the exact form of TabPFN-3's "thinking" variant; TabPFN-3's optimizer, also checked twice with no answer found) — these reflect real gaps in what the sources checked disclose, not guesses. Everything else in the mechanism sections above — including the TabPFN-3 and TabICLv2 sections, after two separate correction passes — was confirmed against the papers' actual Methods/Limitations sections and figures, not just abstracts.

⚠️ **This document has now been substantially corrected twice.** The first pass wrongly claimed TabPFN-3 kept TabPFN v2's cell-level grid attention and didn't engage with TabICL at all — checking Figure 5 directly showed the opposite. A second, more thorough double-check of every remaining row then found: TabPFN-3's "no-padding" mechanism is now via the borrowed TabICL architecture, not v2's tokenizer as first stated; TabPFN-3's regression-uncertainty mechanism is a "bar-distribution" head, not TabICLv2-style quantiles; TabPFN-3 does *not* use curriculum pretraining (confirmed absent, not just unconfirmed); TabPFN-3's distillation and FlashAttention-3/torch.compile usage are now confirmed rather than assumed; TabICLv2's missing-value handling turned out to be mean-imputation only (an admitted limitation, not a native mechanism); TabICLv2 admits no text/relational support; TabICLv2 has used FlashAttention since v1 (an earlier draft wrongly said "not included"); and TabICLv2's "repeated feature grouping" is its own confirmed original contribution, later adopted by TabPFN-3. Treat any claim not explicitly hedged with ⚠️ as reasonably solid now, but this two-round correction history is a reminder that "confirmed" here means "confirmed against the specific passages found," not guaranteed exhaustive verification of the full papers.

⚠️ The field is moving fast: TabICLv2 and TabPFN-3 were both submitted in 2026, within months of "today" (2026-07-09) — newer unpublished or in-review work may already supersede parts of this timeline.

Open questions not resolved by this research pass:
- ❓ Was the architectural convergence at TabPFN-3 abrupt, or are there intermediate signs of TabICL influence in unpublished TabPFN-2.5 revisions?
- ❓ How does TabICLv2 perform against TabPFN-3 specifically on TabICLv2's own benchmarks (TabPFN-3's paper reports beating TabICLv2 on TabPFN-3's large-data benchmark — an independent, symmetric comparison wasn't found)?
- ❓ Exactly how does TabPFN-3-Plus's "thinking" test-time-compute mechanism work internally?
- ❓ Exactly how does TabPFN-3-Plus embed text columns internally (is a separate language model involved)?
- ❓ Does TabICLv2 (or a future TabICL version) reciprocally adopt anything from TabPFN-3's additions (the many-class decoder, orthogonal label embeddings, bar-distribution regression head, or its memory-efficiency tricks)?
- ❓ What optimizer does TabPFN-3 actually use, and what is TabPFN v2's exact regression/missing-value mechanism (both checked multiple times with no answer found in accessible sources)?
