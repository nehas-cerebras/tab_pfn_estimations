TabICLv2: A better, faster, scalable, and open tabular foundation model

Plain-language summary

ELI5

Imagine you have a big spreadsheet and you want a computer to guess the missing answers in one column, using the other columns as clues. Normally you'd have to train a brand-new computer program from scratch for every new spreadsheet. This paper builds a program that has already learned, ahead of time, a very general skill: 'given some example rows with answers, guess the answer for a new row.' You just show it your spreadsheet's examples and it makes good guesses immediately, without any retraining.

The authors trained their program on huge numbers of made-up, computer-generated spreadsheets so it would get good at this general skill before ever seeing a real one. They also made it faster and able to handle much bigger spreadsheets (millions of rows) than earlier versions, while using less computer memory. And they are giving away the program's code and its trained settings for free.

Core claims & contributions

  • The program (called TabICLv2) beats the previous best spreadsheet-guessing programs on two standard test collections, even without any extra tuning for the specific spreadsheet it's given.
  • It matches or beats a competitor model (RealTabPFN-2.5) that was specially adjusted, combined with other models, and further trained on real data for each test, even though TabICLv2 received none of that extra help.
  • It can handle spreadsheets with millions of rows using under 50 GB of graphics-card memory and under 24 GB of regular computer memory, which earlier similar programs struggled to do.
  • It runs noticeably faster than its main rival (up to about 11.8x faster on ordinary computer processors, 10.6x faster on a high-end graphics card) while training on comparatively modest computing resources (about 24.5 GPU-days total).
  • The gains come from three changes: a new way of generating the made-up practice spreadsheets for maximum variety, changes to the program's internal structure (including a new way of weighing information that scales better to big spreadsheets), and switching to a different training method (an optimizer called Muon instead of the usual AdamW).

The full picture

TabICLv2 is a single program, trained once, that can then look at any new spreadsheet-style prediction problem and make predictions immediately, the way a person who has seen thousands of similar puzzles can size up a new one quickly rather than learning from zero each time. During training, instead of using real company data, the authors auto-generate an enormous variety of fake practice spreadsheets with known answers: they randomly build a small web of cause-and-effect relationships, sample random data flowing through it, and read off some of the resulting numbers as 'features' and one as the 'answer to predict.' Because this generator can be tuned to produce almost unlimited variety, the program is exposed to far more variety than any single real dataset could offer, and the authors show this generator design matters more than any other single ingredient in the recipe.

Two other changes make this workable at large scale. First, the program looks at a table in three steps: it summarizes each column on its own, then squashes each row's columns down into one compact description of that row, and finally compares rows to each other to make a prediction — this keeps the amount of computation from exploding as the table grows, unlike some rival designs that compare every cell to every other cell. Second, the internal math used to compare rows to each other (its 'attention' mechanism) was changed so that it doesn't get 'unsure' as the number of rows grows into the thousands or millions — earlier designs tend to spread their attention too thinly across a huge number of rows and lose track of the few that actually matter, and this fix directly targets that failure. Third, the training process itself was switched to a different optimization method (Muon) that reaches a good solution more efficiently than the standard method (AdamW) most such programs use.

Put together, these changes let TabICLv2 be handed a brand-new spreadsheet and immediately produce predictions that are as good as, or better than, competing approaches that require per-dataset tuning, extra fine-tuning on real data, or combining several models together — while also running faster and fitting in less graphics-card memory. The authors are releasing the trained program and its prediction code openly, with the practice-spreadsheet generator and training code to follow, specifically so outside researchers can inspect, build on, and verify how it works rather than relying on a closed system.
